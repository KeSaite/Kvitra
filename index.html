KVEE · trah

kvitra draws on kvit, the old northern root for “white,” and adds the -ra that sets it moving—the pure white and stillness of the first snow as it settles.

I built the name this way; only afterward did I learn it was already a real Norwegian word, meaning “to chirp, to write a letter.” That sense was never intended, yet it was there at the name’s birth, so I keep it here too.

— To me, kvitra is snow; but out in the world it has also been the ringing of first light.

Sound
KVEE-trah, two syllables. It opens on kv—that cold, clean attack the northern tongue keeps for itself.
Root
kvit — the old root for “white” in the Norse languages. They used it for snow, for frost, for bleached wool; it has lived a thousand years on northern tongues.
Ending
-ra — a verb-making tail. It turns “white” from a mere color into something in progress: falling, covering, coming to rest.
Chance
It also happens to be a true Norwegian verb, meaning to chirp — and likewise to write, to send word. A letter landing here is a single birdcall upon the snow.
